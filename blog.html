Hey, I’m a scientist at heart.

So, I’ve just cooked Steak with buttery linguine and it needs improvement. Studying science for most of my life, I think I’ll approach it with the same attitude: write a method, practice, critique the method, write a new method, practice, etc.

Serves 3

Ingredients:

  • 3 sirloin steaks, with high-quality marbling,
  • 2 medium onions, finely chopped,
  • 2 sprigs rosemary,
  • 1 garlic clove,
  • 1 organic beef stock pot,
  • 300g linguine,
  • olive oil, for frying
  • butter, for frying,
  • high-quality extra virgin olive oil.

Method:

  1. Salt the steaks the night before and leave in the fridge.
  2. Heat a griddle or frying pan over a high heat. Splash some water onto the pan and when it sizzles you know it’s ready. Then add the olive oil and, when hot, the onions, rosemary and a pinch of salt; fry until browning.
  3. Meanwhile cook the linguine in salted boiling water, for one minute less than pack instructions. Drain immediately, then put back into the empty pan.
  4. Once the onions begin to brown, grate the garlic in and add the stock pot. Stir continuously so the stock doesn’t catch on the bottom of the pan. Cook until the onions are brown and softened.
  5. Move the onions to one side and in the space add olive oil. Once hot add a dollop of butter followed by the steaks when it begins to sizzle. Cook for 2 minutes each side (rare).
  6. Remove the steaks and leave to rest. Pour the juices and onion from the pan into the linguine, removing the rosemary. If the spaghetti needs loosening add some extra-virgin olive oil.
  7. Slice the steaks and plate on-top of the linguine. Bon appetite .

Critique:

  • Steaks: cooked beautifully. The thicker steak was a bit undercooked so put it on the pan for a bit longer, but the fat and salt used really brought them to life. The fat was a bit soft and I think could have benefited from crisping up. Next time I might remove the fat before cooking, chop up small and fry with the onions so that it goes crispy and mix in with the linguine .
  • Griddle pan: usually great for steaks so I thought why not: I now know why not. Too small to fit everything in, the grooves stop even mixing of the stock and a lot caught on the bottom of the pan. Next time using a normal frying pan.
  • Extra-virgin olive oil: the meal was beautifully fatty, butter smacking from the lips in every bite, however, I think I used too much: olive oil, butter, fat from the steaks, extra-virgin. Next time I would cut the extra-virgin out, reserving some pasta water after draining and using that to loosen the linguine.
  • Colour: the dish was very bland in appearance, the only colour coming from the rare steak. With the high fat content of the meal, I think the dish would benefit from something bitter and I believe swiss/rhubarb chard steamed and turned through the linguine would give it that. The bright colour of chard would also add interest to the look of the dish. Using three rosemary sprigs could also be an idea. That way a sprig could be placed on top of each steak as a garnish.
Overall though, the dish was impressive. It exceeded my expectations and I ended wolfing it down. The steak was cooked beautifully, and the linguine held onto all the juices that ran out. I’ll try and post the new recipe tomorrow!

10 past 01:00, can't sleep and thinking about food, obviously.

I had the following idea for a recipe:

Ingredients:

  • 4 sirloin steaks
  • butter, for frying
  • 360g linguine
  • 2 medium onions, chopped
  • concentrated beef stock
  • red wine sauce (see here )

Method:

  1. 24 hours before cooking, salt the steaks and leave in the fridge.
  2. Cook the linuine following pack instructions in salted water, cooking for one minute less than the packet says.
  3. Meanwhile, melt the butter over a medium-high heat and fry the onions with a few drops of the beef stock until golden-brown. Then add the steaks and cook for 2 minutes each side (medium-rare)
  4. Remove the steaks from the pan and leave to rest. Add the linguine straight from the cooking water to the steak pan and stir around to coat in the juices -add more butter if needed.
  5. Plate the steaks, then add a 'nest' of the linguine next to each and add the red wine sauce so that it forms a pool around the two.
